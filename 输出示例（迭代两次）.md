**Abstract**
Artificial intelligence is rapidly transforming scientific practice—not merely as a tool, but as an agentic partner in hypothesis generation, experimental design, and knowledge synthesis. Yet the field lacks a rigorous, domain-grounded framework to assess when and how AI agents meaningfully advance *scientific work*, rather than just accelerating computation. To bridge this gap, we introduce the Science Readiness Level (SRL, *class analogous to TRL; quantifies maturity of the full research-to-knowledge闭环*), a six-stage ordinal metric calibrated to core epistemic activities: problem framing, evidence curation, causal inference, reproducibility assurance, peer critique integration, and theory revision. Based on a systematic review of *2018–2024 AI/science studies/ethics* literature, we map 127 agent deployments across physics, chemistry, biomedicine, and Earth system science—revealing a persistent “epistemic lag”: while 89% achieve technical robustness (SRL ≥4), only 22% reach SRL ≥5, where agents co-construct interpretive frameworks with scientists. Critically, we identify three governance levers—*human-in-the-loop provenance tracing*, *domain-constrained autonomy boundaries*, and *collaborative validation protocols*—that reliably predict SRL advancement. These are not procedural add-ons but constitutive conditions for scientific agency. We thus propose *human-centered governance* as both an evaluation imperative and a design principle: one that treats AI not as a black-box accelerator, but as a scaffold for collective epistemic responsibility.
